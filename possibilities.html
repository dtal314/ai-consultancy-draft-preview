What moves these numbers in practice

The levers are not settings. They're decisions.

Each of these sits on the Understand → Adopt → Build spine, and each one is where a modelled number either becomes real or quietly doesn't.

Understand

Choose the problem before the bot

Most AI disappointment starts with a tool looking for a job. Baseline one workflow first, what it costs now, who touches it, where it stalls, and settle policy, security, liability, training and ROI ownership before anything is adopted.

If you can't baseline it, you can't model it.

Adopt

Train people who can carry it

General AI literacy across the firm, deeper role-specific literacy where the work actually happens, and named AiT champions who organize and keep track of use. Adoption is staged as the firm ramps, not announced.

The uptake lever is a people number, not a software number.

Adopt

Validate output, not only speed

Measure review and rework, not just generation time, a fast wrong answer is a slower workflow. Human quality control stays. So do security, copyright, ethics, and professional liability review.

Time reduction that skips checking isn't time reduction.

Build

Build to your standards

Once a bottleneck is defined and measured, targeted apps, agents and POWER tools can be built around your own standards, your data, and your hard-won lessons, rather than bending your process to fit someone else's idea of how you should work.

Build for a bottleneck you measured. Never for a demo.

Why the numbers keep moving

The cost floor under all of this is not stable.

Any model you build today sits on top of economics that are still shifting. That is an argument for measuring your own pilot rather than trusting a benchmark, including ours.

  • Tokens are the unit. Tokens are units of data processed by AI models to generate outputs; each task consumes a share of them. Larger models and harder tasks cost more, cost varies by model, and rising cost has already changed when and how people use these tools. Source, Dan to provide
  • Hardware is moving under it. The deck reports chip efficiency up 150× since 2023, and characterises the direction as 50× faster and 35× cheaper, pushing token cost down, compute up, and latency down. Source, Dan to provide
  • The infrastructure is early. “Building AI infrastructure stage: similar to the Internet from 1991 to 1998.” Source, Dan to provide
  • There is an environmental ledger. Data-center water use, “Data Center Alley” carbon emissions, and energy projections are real considerations, and closed-loop cooling cuts water consumption versus the evaporative systems used previously. For landscape architects this is not a footnote. Source, Dan to provide

Insourcing. Get comfortable with the word. With AI-assisted development, firms can increasingly build targeted tools and workflows in-house, tuned to their own standards, their own data, their own hard-won lessons. No more waiting eighteen months for a vendor to half-implement a feature request. No more bending your process to fit someone else's idea of how you should work.

Common questions

About this model.

How do you calculate the ROI of AI in an architecture or engineering firm?

Start from one specific repeatable workflow rather than the whole firm. Multiply the people performing it by their hours per week and working weeks per year to get baseline annual hours. Apply the share of that workflow you actually complete with AI, and the measured time reduction on the assisted work, to get hours reclaimed. Multiply by a fully loaded hourly cost for capacity value, then subtract one-time implementation and twelve months of recurring AI cost. Capacity value is not booked revenue or cash savings.

What is a fully loaded hourly cost?

A finance-approved internal cost per hour for a person, including salary, benefits, taxes and overhead. It is not the billing rate charged to a client. Using a billing rate is the most common way these models get inflated, it prices the hour at what you sell it for rather than what it costs you.

Is there a standard productivity benchmark for AI in AEC?

No universal AEC productivity benchmark is assumed here. Any percentage you use should come from a measured pilot in your own firm, compared like for like, including the time spent prompting, checking and reworking output. That is why both sliders start at zero and why no scenario on this page is labelled “conservative” or “typical”, we have no credible basis for those labels, so we don't use them.

Model it, then go measure it.

A number on this page is a hypothesis. The next step is a bounded pilot on the workflow you just modelled.
