Responsible use

The question is not whether AI will be used. It is how it will be used responsibly.

Adoption is not a future event. It is already underway — often informally. This page is the framework we use to decide what AI touches, how much review the work earns, and who stays accountable for the result.

Drawn from “AI in Design Practice and Education: Opportunities, Risks, and Responsibilities” — CLARB Annual Meeting 2026 keynote, Jenn Becker.

Risk · convincing errors

Hallucinations can look professional.

This is the failure mode that matters in a licensed profession. Not obvious nonsense — plausible, well-formatted, confident nonsense that passes a glance.

Practice hygiene

A responsible-use checklist.

Take it. Use it. It is twelve questions, and it is the cheapest risk control available to a design firm right now.

Before

  • Is AI appropriate for this task?
  • What information will be shared?
  • What could happen if the output is wrong?
  • Who is qualified to review it?

During

  • Are assumptions and sources documented?
  • Are outputs treated as suggestions, not facts?
  • Are checkpoints built into the workflow?
  • Is meaningful human control maintained?

Before delivery

  • Has the output been independently verified?
  • Is disclosure appropriate?
  • Is it consistent with contracts, policies, ethics, and regulations?
  • Is a named person accountable for the final decision?

The last question is the one that decides whether any of the others happened.

Common questions

Asked plainly, answered plainly.

When is AI helpful in design work, and when is it not?

It depends on two things: the consequence of an error, and how easy the output is to verify. Low consequence and easy to verify — draft emails, meeting summaries, file renaming, formatting — is the best starting ground. High consequence but easy to verify, such as quantity takeoffs checked against the model or code lookups confirmed in the code itself, can be used with rigorous verification. Low consequence but hard to verify, like ideation imagery or precedent brainstorms, is useful if treated as suggestion. High consequence and hard to verify — grading and drainage decisions, structural or accessibility details, sealed documents — is least appropriate for uncritical use. The more consequential and difficult to verify the output, the less appropriate it is for uncritical AI use.

Can AI output look correct but be wrong?

Yes. Polished is not the same as correct. AI-generated technical content can combine an incorrect dimension, missing information such as no edge restraint or joint spec, an invented standard whose cited number does not exist, and assumed site information the model cannot know — while still looking entirely professional. Constructability, materials, and site conditions still require a professional.

Can an AI QA/QC checker catch AI mistakes?

Not reliably. A confident, polished AI output checked by an AI checker that confidently agrees still requires human professional review. The two systems can share assumptions, share training limitations, miss context, produce false positives and false negatives, and give confident explanations of wrong results.

How much review does AI-assisted work need?

Match review to risk. Low-risk communication support needs basic human review. Internal option generation needs comparison and source checking. Client-facing material needs design-team review plus appropriate disclosure. Technical documentation needs qualified disciplinary review plus established QA/QC. Health, safety and welfare work, code, and sealed work need full professional judgment and jurisdiction-appropriate review. Review effort should climb the ladder with consequence, not with convenience.

What is bounded autonomy in an AI workflow?

Bounded autonomy is a multi-step workflow that runs inside explicit limits. It is the fourth stage after manual work where a human does the task, AI assistance where a human directs each step, and automation where defined tasks run on their own. Autonomous workflows earn their bounds through defined limits, checkpoints, logs, human approval, and a clear stop mechanism.

Does AI make design firms look more alike?

It can. Shared inputs converge on shared outputs: the same models, same prompts, same image styles, same datasets, same defaults and same optimization goals produce homogenized imagery, generic language, repeated design solutions, reduced local specificity, and loss of firm identity. What still differentiates a firm is local knowledge, community relationships, design judgment, proprietary processes, customized tools, and a clear point of view.
