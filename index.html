Why this matters

Experimentation is not the problem. Repeatability is.

If your team is testing AI, the gap is everything around the test: what is allowed, what stays private, who carries the liability, who learns the practice, and whether any of it creates measurable value.

Five things every firm has to address

  1. Policy and use

    What is allowed, what is disclosed, and what gets noted about how AI was used. This will change over time, which is exactly why it needs an owner rather than an opinion.

  2. Security and privacy

    Which tools see your client data, on what terms, and where it goes afterward. Check the pro policy — coverage is not what most people assume it is.

  3. Liability and copyright

    End users are not protected by default. End-user agreements place liability on the user, and companies are suing the AI companies. AI does not replace professional liability risk.

  4. Worker training and retention

    General literacy across the firm, deeper literacy for specific people, and a way to keep both current as the tools move.

  5. ROI

    Budget, cost, investment. Token costs vary by model and task, and that variance has already changed when and how people use these tools.

This is not legal advice. Firms should review liability, copyright and security questions with their own counsel and IT.

What changes when this work is done well

The tools stop being a side project.

  • Policy and use are written down, owned, and revisited
  • Security and privacy questions are asked before a tool is adopted, not after
  • Liability and copyright exposure is understood rather than assumed away
  • Literacy exists firmwide, and deeper literacy exists where the work is
  • Named AiT champions organize and keep track of AI use inside the firm
  • Pilots are measured, and road maps point at real bottlenecks

One spine, three stages.

Every engagement sits somewhere on it. Most firms want to start at the third stage. Almost none of them should.

Stage one

Understand

What these tools are, in plain language. Which bots do what, which ones may merit a subscription and why, and what a token actually is — because it is the unit your costs are denominated in.

Literacy across the firm. Then literacy where it counts.

Stage two

Adopt

Governed use. Policy, security, privacy, liability. Bounded pilots on real work, measured honestly. Training for the people who will carry it, including designated AiT champions who organize and track use.

One workflow, done repeatably, beats ten demos.

Stage three

Build

Selective insourcing. Custom apps aimed at defined bottlenecks, agents and automation, visualization pipelines, and tools built to your standards — not a vendor's idea of your standards.

Only for a bottleneck you have already measured.

Common questions

Answered plainly.

What must an AEC firm address before adopting AI at scale?

Five things: policy and use, security and privacy, liability and copyright, worker training and retention, and ROI. Most firms begin experimenting before any of the five has an owner. That is not a disaster — it is normal — but it is the gap that turns promising pilots into stalled ones.

Does AI remove professional liability risk?

No. AI does not replace professional liability risk. End users are not protected by default, end-user agreements place liability on users, and firms still have to address liability, copyright, ethics, security and privacy themselves. Companies are actively suing the AI companies. Review this with your own counsel — nothing here is legal advice.

What is an AiT?

An AiT is a designated in-house AI trainee or champion. A specific named person inside a firm who receives deeper role-specific AI training and helps organize and keep track of how AI is used across the firm. General literacy tells everyone what the tools are; an AiT is who owns it once the training ends.
