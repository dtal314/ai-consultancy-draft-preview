Why this matters

We teach you the understanding you need, and how to make it repeatable.

Immersion, case studies, guides and years of navigating a brand new ecosystem has taught us to control costs, where Ai fails, how to assess tools and services wisely and ensure safety within our firm.

Five things every firm has to address

  1. Understand the AI ecosystem

    We explain the basics and the details, from the big picture down to the micro uses. Which bots do what, which ones may merit a subscription and why, what a token actually is, and where each tool genuinely belongs in design work.

    You are not behind, wise adoption is slow and steady.

  2. Security, privacy and policy

    Which tools see your client data, on what terms, and where it goes afterward, and then what is allowed, what is disclosed, and what gets noted about how AI was used. Your firm staff is using. Understanding in progress. Adoption is key. This changes over time, which is exactly why it needs an owner rather than an opinion.

  3. Worker training and retention

    General literacy across the firm, deeper literacy for the specific people who will carry it, and a way to keep both current as the tools move. This is the part that decides whether anything sticks.

  4. Liability and copyright

    End users are not protected by default. End-user agreements place liability on the user, and companies are suing the AI companies. AI does not replace professional liability risk.

    TBH, nobody is protected, understanding leads to better risk assessment.

  5. ROI

    Budget, cost, investment. Token costs vary by model and task, and that variance has already changed when and how people use these tools.

    WHAT IS A TOKEN ANYWAY... exactly, we Understand.

This is not legal advice. Firms should review liability, copyright and security questions with their own counsel and IT.

Stage two · what changes when this work is done well

We Adopt.

Adoption does not happen overnight. Busy schedules, resistance and other priorities are real drivers to slow or aborted integration. We simplify the process by sharing our successes and mistakes, but most of all how to empower staff to duplicate our approach and results.

  1. Foundations make AI both responsible and efficient

    The two are the same job. A team that understands what a tool is doing uses it on the right work, checks it in the right places, and stops wasting time on the things it was never going to do well.

  2. The assistant drafts. A person decides.

    It organizes, compares, prepares and drafts. Judgment, and accountability for the result, stay with a named human, every time.

  3. The same task works twice

    It produces the same quality on a Tuesday as it did on the Friday it first worked. Anything that only works when one person runs it is a trick, not a workflow.

  4. Someone owns it

    Named AiTs, AI Technology people, organize and keep track of AI use inside the firm. Literacy exists firmwide, and deeper literacy exists where the work actually is.

  5. The risky questions are asked first

    Security, privacy, liability and copyright get settled before a tool is adopted rather than after it has already touched a client's data.

  6. It is measured, not assumed

    Pilots are measured honestly, including the ones that didn't work, and road maps point at real bottlenecks instead of whatever demoed well.

Common questions

Answered plainly.

What must an AEC firm address before adopting AI at scale?

Five things: policy and use, security and privacy, liability and copyright, worker training and retention, and ROI. Most firms begin experimenting before any of the five has an owner. That is not a disaster, it is normal, but it is the gap that turns promising pilots into stalled ones.

Does AI remove professional liability risk?

No. AI does not replace professional liability risk. End users are not protected by default, end-user agreements place liability on users, and firms still have to address liability, copyright, ethics, security and privacy themselves. Companies are actively suing the AI companies. Review this with your own counsel, nothing here is legal advice.

What is an AiT?

An AiT is an AI Technology person, a dedicated in-house R&D person or group, given the full scope of current understanding and tools, whose job is to propel the firm forward and keep AI adoption alive from the inside. General literacy tells everyone what the tools are; an AiT is who owns it once the training ends. Building that team is Stage Three.
